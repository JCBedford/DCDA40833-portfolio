Lab 04: Exploratory Data Analysis in Tableau

Interactive Dashboard

Reflection and Analysis

Data Preparation

When I first uploaded the dataset into Tableau Public, I encountered several formatting issues. Some crime rate columns were automatically classified as text rather than numeric measures, which prevented me from creating histograms and properly sorting the top ten states in a bar chart. I manually adjusted the data types within the data source tab to ensure the fields were treated as numeric measures. I also reviewed column headers for consistency and filtered the dataset to focus on the most recent year when building the state comparison. After these adjustments, the dataset functioned correctly and allowed for smooth visualization development. Also, when posting my visualizations, I first made it into a dashboard. but I found that the dashboard was not working when I posted it, so I had to post the visualizations separately.

Design Choices

I created three primary visualizations to examine crime patterns from multiple analytical angles. First, I built a line graph showing the trend of crime rates over time. I used year as the dimension and crime rate as the measure in order to identify long term increases or decreases. A line graph was the most appropriate choice because it clearly displays temporal change and allows for easy identification of peaks, declines, and overall trajectory. This visualization provides historical context and establishes whether crime trends are stable, increasing, or decreasing over time.

In addition to the time trend, I created two bar charts to examine state level differences. The first bar chart displays the top ten states with the highest violent crime rates. I used state as the dimension and violent crime rate as the measure. This makes geographic concentration immediately visible. The second bar chart compares those same ten states using property crime rates. By holding the states constant and changing the measure, I was able to compare whether states with high violent crime also experience similarly high property crime. This comparative structure allows for a clearer analysis of whether different types of crime cluster together or diverge across states. Together, these three visualizations move from national trends to state level concentration and then to cross crime comparison.

Expected Patterns

Based on prior knowledge of crime trends, I expected property crime rates to be higher than violent crime rates across states. The visualizations confirmed this expectation, as property crime values were consistently larger. I also anticipated that the crime trend would look the way it does. It is concurrent with the rise of the war on drugs which is a prominent piece of history taught in many criminal justice courses. The line graph shows a significant increase in crime rates starting in the late 1970s, peaking in the early 1990s, and then declining steadily through the 2000s. This pattern aligns with the historical context of drug-related crime and subsequent policy responses.

Surprises and Outliers

One notable surprise was the degree of variation within the top ten states. Although I expected differences, the gap between the highest and lowest states in that group was more substantial than anticipated. Additionally, the violent crime bar chart showed tighter clustering among most states than I initially expected, suggesting that extreme cases may receive disproportionate public attention compared to their statistical frequency. The property crime distribution was also more concentrated than predicted.

Questions for Further Investigation

These patterns raise important questions. What socioeconomic or structural factors contribute to states that consistently rank highest in violent crime? How do reporting standards influence cross state comparisons? Would incorporating variables such as poverty rates, urbanization levels, or educational attainment shift the interpretation of these patterns? A longitudinal analysis could also reveal whether rankings remain stable over time or fluctuate significantly.

Tool Evaluation

I have used Tableau once before in my Data Analysis and Visualization course, and while I was able to use it in more detail this time, I still found it to be a user-friendly tool for creating interactive visualizations. Tableau Public proved to be an effective tool for exploratory analysis. Its drag and drop interface allowed me to quickly test different measures and dimensions, while the dashboard feature enabled me to combine multiple visualizations into a cohesive presentation. However, data cleaning options are limited compared to tools like R or Python, and advanced statistical analysis is not easily integrated. Despite these limitations, Tableau excels at rapid pattern identification and professional presentation, making it well suited for exploratory visualization and portfolio development.
